Cravings for tobacco are temporary and usually last between 5 and 10 minutes. When a craving occurs, delay smoking and focus on another activity until the urge passes.

The 4D technique helps manage cravings: Delay smoking for a few minutes, take Deep breaths slowly, Drink water, and Do something else to distract yourself.

Morning cravings are common for people who smoke regularly. Drinking water, brushing your teeth, or going for a short walk can help reduce the urge to smoke.

Stress is a common trigger for smoking. Relaxation techniques such as deep breathing, meditation, or short walks can help manage stress without using tobacco.

Nicotine replacement therapy such as nicotine gum, patches, or lozenges can help reduce withdrawal symptoms and cravings.

Nicotine gum should be chewed slowly and then placed between the cheek and gum so that nicotine can be absorbed gradually through the mouth.

Triggers are situations or emotions that make someone want to smoke. Common triggers include stress, drinking alcohol, social gatherings, and boredom.

Avoid situations where others are smoking during the early stages of quitting. Being around smokers can increase the urge to smoke.

If a craving occurs, try changing your environment. Move to another room, go outside for fresh air, or start another activity.

Many people smoke out of habit during specific routines such as after meals, while drinking tea or coffee, or when talking with friends.

Replacing smoking with another activity such as chewing gum, eating healthy snacks, or walking can help break the habit.

Withdrawal symptoms from nicotine may include irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, increased appetite, and strong cravings.

Withdrawal symptoms usually peak within the first few days after quitting and gradually decrease over time.

Drinking water frequently can help reduce cravings and keep the mouth busy.

Deep breathing exercises can calm the body and reduce the urge to smoke.

Taking a short walk or doing light physical activity can help reduce stress and distract from cravings.

Many people find that writing down their reasons for quitting smoking helps maintain motivation during difficult moments.

Support from friends, family, or healthcare professionals can improve the chances of successfully quitting smoking.

Planning ahead for difficult situations such as parties or social events can help prevent relapse.

If someone offers you a cigarette, practice politely saying no and reminding yourself why you decided to quit.

Relapse can happen during the quitting process. Instead of feeling discouraged, treat it as a learning experience and continue trying to quit.

Alcohol can increase the urge to smoke, so reducing alcohol consumption may help prevent cravings.

Keeping your hands busy with activities such as drawing, writing, or using a stress ball can help reduce the urge to smoke.

Eating healthy snacks like fruits, nuts, or vegetables can help manage increased appetite during nicotine withdrawal.

Sleep problems may occur after quitting smoking, but these usually improve after a few weeks.

Exercise can help improve mood and reduce nicotine cravings.

Avoid keeping cigarettes, lighters, or ashtrays nearby when trying to quit smoking.

Reward yourself for milestones such as staying smoke-free for a day, a week, or a month.

Talking to someone about your cravings can help reduce the intensity of the urge.

If you slip and smoke a cigarette, it does not mean you have failed. Many people attempt quitting several times before succeeding.

Nicotine addiction is both physical and psychological. Addressing both aspects can improve success in quitting.

Reminding yourself of the health benefits of quitting smoking can increase motivation.

Within weeks of quitting smoking, breathing and circulation often improve.

Quitting smoking reduces the risk of lung disease, heart disease, and many types of cancer.

Making a daily plan for managing cravings can make the quitting process easier.

Keeping a diary of triggers and cravings can help identify patterns and develop better coping strategies.
